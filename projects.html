Wet Lab Project

Backgound

Gastrointestinal (GI) cancer refer to malignant growths along the digestive tract, including the esophagus, stomach, liver, pancreas, small intestines, and colon. The biggest problem with GI cancers is that they rarely cause symptoms during the early stages of malignancy, making them very hard to detect. Symptoms often only appear once the disease has progressed to an advanced stage.1 The treatments available for patients who have been diagnosed with a late stage GI cancer are surgery, radiation, and/or chemotherapy.2 These are invasive and painful options, and often the five-year survival rates are low, with 8% for pancreatic cancer and 25% for gastric cancer.3 Bowel resection is the most common form of surgery, where parts of the colon are removed with the tumour.7 Extensive radiation treatment is administered as part of chemoradiation therapy for patients with rectal carcinoma. Long-term radiation therapy is administered preoperatively and postoperatively but is known to cause long-term bowel dysfunction for patients.10 Chemotherapy regimens such as 5-fluorouracil, capecitabine, oxaliplatin, irinotecan, raltitrexed, and gemcitazine are also used to varying effects.8,9 We propose a novel treatment option for gastrointestinal cancers that is non-invasive and highly selective for tumorous cells. We are engineering a strain of gut bacteria to be able to recognize and attach to cancer cells. Then, based on the density of these bacteria around the tumour cells, the bacteria will signal to the immune system, allowing it to destroy the malignant cells.

The Her2 Protein

Human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) is involved in the pathological development of gastric and gastroesophageal cancer. Its role is to promote cell proliferation and suppress apoptosis, which may facilitate uncontrolled cell growth and tumorigenesis. Overexpression of HER2 is associated with increased risk of local growth and metastasis.4 HER2 has the potential to serve as a marker for tumours, one which bacteria can recognize. The designed Ankyrin repeat protein (DARPin) G3 binds HER2 with high affinity.5 Expression of DARPin G3 in bacteria for the binding of HER2 in tumour cell cultures have shown high selectivity, as well as a good expression yield of the DARPin.6
